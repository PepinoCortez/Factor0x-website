Whitepaper
Factor0x

1. Overview

Factor0x is a cross-border invoice financing / trade receivables financing platform that helps businesses access liquidity against verified B2B invoices and short-term receivables.

The platform connects:

• SMEs and mid-market companies with verified invoices;

• investors and capital providers seeking an opportunity to participate in the financing of verified receivables;

• SPV / licensed partner / factoring provider;

• digital-asset capital providers, where legally permitted;

• AI infrastructure for invoice verification and risk scoring;

• compliance, KYB / KYC and AML framework;

• Web3 smart-contract layer for investor participation accounting, deal status transparency and distribution logic.

Factor0x is not a speculative token project. It is an infrastructure platform for financing real B2B receivables with a Web3 layer for transparency, accounting and automation.

UAE / Dubai is considered the first operating, commercial and regulatory hub for:

• first invoices;

• first SMEs / borrowers;

• first UAE / GCC obligors;

• first pilot transactions;

• crypto-native liquidity infrastructure;

• legal / regulatory pathway review;

• regulated digital asset / conversion infrastructure where required.

Singapore and Southeast Asia are considered later as a second layer for structuring, banking, compliance and regional scaling.


2. Mission

The mission of Factor0x is to make the financing of verified receivables faster, more transparent and more accessible by combining:

• verified B2B invoices;

• AI-powered invoice verification;

• risk scoring / underwriting;

• structured capital deployment;

• fiat and crypto-native liquidity;

• smart-contract-based accounting;

• institutional-grade compliance and reporting.

Factor0x aims to turn verified receivables into a transparent, controlled and financeable asset class.


3. Problem

Many SMEs earn revenue before they receive cash.

In sectors such as logistics, trade, distribution, construction, import/export and B2B services, companies often issue invoices with payment terms of 30–120 days.

This creates a recurring gap:

• the service has been performed or goods have been delivered;

• the invoice has been issued;

• the debtor / obligor will pay later;

• the business needs liquidity now.

Traditional factoring and invoice financing have several limitations.

3.1 Limited access for SMEs

Many SMEs do not meet the requirements of banks or traditional factoring providers.

3.2 Slow approval process

Manual underwriting, document review and credit analysis often take too much time.

3.3 Lack of transparency

Investors and capital providers often have limited visibility into invoice quality, document status, debtor risk, repayment progress and portfolio performance.

3.4 Complexity of cross-border financing

International invoice financing is complicated by legal, banking, currency and compliance factors.

3.5 Underutilized crypto liquidity

Digital-asset markets may become an additional source of liquidity for financing short-term B2B receivables, subject to applicable regulation.

Factor0x does not view this segment as a public fundraising channel from an unrestricted investor audience, but as a potential source of structured liquidity that may be connected through a legally permissible model, compliance procedures and regulated partners where required.

The key objective is to create a clear and defensible framework in which digital-asset liquidity can be directed into the financing of real trade transactions without breaching requirements related to investor onboarding, AML/KYC, custody, offering restrictions and virtual asset activity.

9. Transaction Workflow

9.1 Borrower Flow

1. Borrower uploads the invoice and supporting documents.

2. Factor0x performs KYB and initial document intake.

3. The AI engine extracts and verifies invoice data.

4. The obligor is reviewed and risk-scored.

5. Supporting documents are reviewed.

6. The invoice receives the status approved, rejected or manual review.

7. Financing terms are proposed.

8. The transaction is listed privately or to eligible investors.

9. Capital is raised through the approved structure.

10. The SME receives financing.

11. The debtor pays the invoice into a controlled account.

12. Repayment is distributed according to the legal and platform logic.

9.2 Investor Flow

1. Investor completes KYC / KYB where required / subject to onboarding rules.

2. Investor completes wallet screening and source-of-funds checks.

3. Investor sees available invoice-backed opportunities.

4. Investor reviews indicative economics.

5. Investor contributes eligible assets through approved channels, subject to legal and compliance checks.

6. Investor receives digital confirmation of participation.

7. Investor tracks deal status.

8. Investor receives distributions according to transaction terms, subject to risk and legal documentation.

9.3 Repayment Flow

1. Debtor / obligor pays the invoice.

2. Funds arrive at the SPV / licensed partner / designated collection account.

3. The receipt is reconciled according to approved transaction documents.

4. Transaction proceeds and investor allocations are calculated according to approved transaction documents.

5. Applicable platform and partner fees are deducted if provided by the transaction structure.

6. Distributions are made in accordance with the approved transaction documents.

7. Deal status is updated to Repaid, Overdue or Default.

11. Investor Participation Model

Deal-by-deal / asset-by-asset model

Factor0x is considering a model where investors participate not in a broad manager-driven public pool, but in individual invoice-backed opportunities or pre-defined transactions.

In this model:

• investors see specific invoice-backed opportunities;

• investors choose whether to participate in a specific transaction;

• each opportunity has disclosed parameters: borrower, obligor, amount, tenor, risk tier, verification status and indicative economics;

• capital is not transferred into a broad manager-driven pool without selection of specific assets;

• the legal form of investor participation is defined by approved transaction documents and is subject to legal review.

Disclosed parameters may include:

• borrower;

• obligor;

• invoice amount;

• maturity date;

• expected return;

• risk tier;

• verification status;

• supporting document status;

• jurisdiction;

• repayment source.

13. Risk Management Framework

13.1 Credit Risk

Credit risk arises if the debtor does not pay the invoice.

Risk mitigation measures:

• obligor analysis;

• debtor acknowledgment;

• invoice verification;

• concentration limits;

• risk tiering;

• potential reserve mechanisms;

• manual review for high-risk invoices.

13.2 Fraud Risk

Fraud risk includes fake invoices, duplicate invoices, altered documents, disputed receivables or double financing.

Risk mitigation measures:

• AI document checks;

• duplicate detection;

• supporting document validation;

• debtor confirmation;

• KYB checks;

• adverse media screening;

• manual review.

13.3 Liquidity Risk

Liquidity risk arises if investors expect capital to be returned before invoices are repaid.

Risk mitigation measures:

• short-duration assets;

• maturity matching between invoice tenor and investor expectations;

• no guaranteed return or early redemption unless expressly documented;

• clear investor disclosure;

• repayment status tracking;

• reserve policy, if provided by the transaction structure.

13.4 Operational Risk

Operational risks include:

• incorrect document processing;

• incorrect scoring;

• data privacy breach;

• incorrect payment allocation;

• admin key compromise;

• smart contract bugs;

• failed integration;

• delayed reconciliation.

Risk mitigation measures:

• RBAC;

• audit trail;

• encrypted document storage;

• access control;

• reconciliation controls;

• smart contract audit;

• incident response plan.
